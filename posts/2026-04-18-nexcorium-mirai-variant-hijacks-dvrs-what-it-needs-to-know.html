Nexcorium Mirai Variant Hijacks DVRs: What IT Needs to Know

By AI Security & Compliance Desk April 18, 2026

Source: The Hacker News

The digital landscape is a constant battleground, and even the most unassuming devices can become weapons in the hands of threat actors. A new variant of the notorious Mirai botnet, dubbed Nexcorium, is now actively exploiting vulnerabilities in commonly deployed hardware, turning surveillance systems and home routers into unwitting participants in massive distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks.

According to recent findings from Fortinet FortiGuard Labs and Palo Alto Networks Unit 42, highlighted by The Hacker News, this latest evolution of Mirai targets a specific command injection vulnerability, CVE-2024-3721, found in TBK DVR devices. While rated a medium-severity (CVSS score: 6.3) flaw, its impact is far from trivial, allowing attackers to hijack these devices and integrate them into the Nexcorium botnet. Compounding the problem, end-of-life (EoL) TP-Link Wi-Fi routers are also falling victim, demonstrating a persistent threat vector against unsupported hardware.

The Enduring Mirai Legacy and Nexcorium's Evolution

Mirai, first discovered in 2016, revolutionized the IoT threat landscape by weaponizing insecure internet-connected devices. Its ability to scan for and exploit default credentials and known vulnerabilities to build vast botnets for DDoS attacks has made it a persistent menace. Nexcorium represents a concerning refinement of this model. Instead of relying solely on brute-force or widely known exploits, this variant specifically targets CVE-2024-3721 in TBK DVRs. This command injection vulnerability, if unpatched, provides a straightforward path for attackers to execute arbitrary code, gaining control over the device. Once compromised, these DVRs, often deployed in business and public settings, become nodes in a distributed network capable of overwhelming target servers and services with malicious traffic.

The Peril of End-of-Life Devices

The inclusion of EoL TP-Link Wi-Fi routers in Nexcorium's target list underscores a critical and often overlooked security gap. Devices that no longer receive firmware updates or security patches become perpetual vulnerabilities. For IT professionals and compliance officers, this presents a significant challenge:

  • Unpatched Flaws: EoL devices retain all known and undiscovered vulnerabilities, making them easy targets for threat actors.
  • No Vendor Support: Manufacturers cease to provide security advisories or patches, leaving organizations on their own.
  • Compliance Risks: Operating unsupported hardware can lead to non-compliance with various industry regulations and data protection standards.
  • Network Entry Points: A compromised EoL router, even if seemingly isolated, can serve as a beachhead for attackers to pivot deeper into an internal network.

The economics of replacing functional but outdated hardware often clash with security imperatives. However, the cost of a data breach, service disruption, or reputational damage far outweighs the investment in secure, supported infrastructure.

Fortifying Your Defenses Against IoT Botnets

Combating threats like Nexcorium requires a multi-layered approach, focusing on proactive measures and diligent management of all network-connected devices.

Here are key strategies for IT and security teams:

  • Asset Inventory & Lifecycle Management: Maintain a comprehensive inventory of all IoT and network devices. Crucially, track their support lifecycle and plan for timely replacement or secure decommissioning of EoL hardware.
  • Patch Management: Prioritize patching for known vulnerabilities. For TBK DVRs, ensure any available patches for CVE-2024-3721 are applied immediately. Regularly check vendor advisories for all active devices.
  • Strong Authentication: Enforce unique, complex passwords for all devices, moving beyond default credentials. Implement multi-factor authentication (MFA) wherever possible.
  • Network Segmentation: Isolate IoT devices on separate network segments or VLANs. This limits their ability to interact with critical internal systems if compromised and prevents lateral movement by attackers.
  • Traffic Monitoring: Implement robust network monitoring to detect unusual outbound traffic patterns, which could indicate a device has been co-opted into a botnet. Look for spikes in bandwidth usage or connections to known malicious IPs.
  • Regular Security Audits: Conduct periodic security assessments and penetration tests on IoT deployments to identify and remediate vulnerabilities before they are exploited.

The Nexcorium variant is a stark reminder that the battle for cybersecurity extends far beyond traditional endpoints. Every internet-connected device, from high-end servers to seemingly innocuous DVRs and routers, represents a potential vector for attack. For IT professionals, security teams, and compliance officers, the message is clear: vigilance, proactive management, and a commitment to secure device lifecycles are not optional. Ignoring the security posture of even a single device can open the door to widespread compromise, turning your infrastructure into a weapon against others, or worse, against your own operations. Stay informed, stay patched, and secure your entire digital perimeter.
